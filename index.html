History of Nuclear testing:

Nuclear testing in the Pacific region began in the 1950s. Both Britain and the US conducted tests.                                                                                                                                                           The US by 1962, when it stopped testing in the Pacific, had exploded 106 bombs on Bikini Atoll and Enewetak,                                                                                                                                 From 1952 to 1962 Britain exploded a total of 21 bombs - in the Australian outback, and on Montebello and Christmas Islands.                                                                                                 France began testing in the Pacific in 1966, and by 1996 had tested 193 nuclear bombs.                   By the time France stopped testing in the Pacific in early 1996, over 250 nuclear detonations had occurred.

New Zealand’s choices of direction (after WW2) seem to have been:

1. Assert independent efforts to safeguard the Pacific area where NZ could be a strong influence.

2. Maintain the commonwealth connection and stay part of Britain’s global strategy.

3. Promote international peacekeeping by supporting a strong United Nations which could remove the causes of war.

4. Choose sides in the growing Cold War conflict between the USSR and the USA.

5. Try to wipe out causes of war with aid and development programmes.

6. Join military defence pacts to gain military support just in case war did become a threat.

7. Find another major protector – swap Britain for the USA.

During WW2, NZ was eager to help develop the atomic bomb. However, by 1975, the government could no longer put off a decisive decision on whether or not to build a nuclear reactor on Kaipara Harbour. By this time, concerns about health risks of the nuclear industry had made it less desirable.

France carried out its first testing in Mururoa Atoll in 1966, it provided a focus for NZ’s anti nuclear campaign.In 1971, a poll showed that 82% of NZers were opposed to French testing in the Pacific. So when Labour came to power in 1972, Norman Kirk challenged French nuclear testing in the pacific.

In 1973, in combination with the Australian Labour Government, NZ took France to the World Court for its nuclear testing. The court found against France and ordered it to stop its testing programme. However, France ignored this order. NZ discovered that its allies, America and Britain were unwilling to take any action to help NZ and Australia.

Since America and Britain didn’t help us, the nuclear movement begane to take on a nationalistic flavour. A petition against French nuclear testing got over 81,000 signatures while peace organisations such as Peacemedia and Greenpeace formed.
